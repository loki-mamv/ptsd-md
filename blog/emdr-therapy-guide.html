EMDR Therapy: How It Works and What the Research Shows

Published February 7, 2026 · 10 min read

Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing, known as EMDR, is one of the most extensively researched treatments for PTSD. Since psychologist Francine Shapiro developed it in 1987, it has gone from a controversial newcomer to one of the most widely recommended trauma therapies in the world. The World Health Organization, the American Psychological Association, the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs, and the U.K.'s National Institute for Health and Care Excellence all recommend it as a frontline PTSD treatment.

Despite its strong evidence base, EMDR remains one of the least understood therapies among the general public. Many people hear about it and wonder: How can moving your eyes back and forth heal trauma? The answer is more nuanced and more interesting than the question implies.

How EMDR Was Discovered

In 1987, Francine Shapiro noticed that certain eye movements seemed to reduce the intensity of disturbing thoughts she was experiencing during a walk. She began systematically testing this observation, first with volunteers and then in controlled studies. Her initial randomized controlled trial, published in the Journal of Traumatic Stress in 1989, showed significant reductions in PTSD symptoms among trauma survivors who received EMDR compared to a control group.

Early critics questioned the mechanism and the results. Over the following three decades, more than 30 randomized controlled trials have been conducted on EMDR for PTSD, making it one of the most researched psychotherapy approaches in the trauma field. The evidence has consistently supported its effectiveness.

The Eight Phases of EMDR

EMDR is not simply "waving fingers in front of someone's face." It is a structured, eight-phase treatment protocol that typically unfolds over 6 to 12 sessions.

Phase 1: History and Treatment Planning

The therapist conducts a thorough assessment of the patient's trauma history, current symptoms, and treatment goals. Together, they identify the specific traumatic memories that will be targeted during processing. This phase may take one to two sessions.

Phase 2: Preparation

The therapist explains the EMDR process and teaches the patient self-regulation techniques—such as the "safe place" visualization or controlled breathing—that can be used to manage distress during and between sessions. Building a strong therapeutic relationship is central to this phase.

Phase 3: Assessment

For each target memory, the therapist helps the patient identify the most vivid visual image associated with it, the negative belief about themselves connected to the memory (such as "I am powerless"), the desired positive belief (such as "I have strength"), the emotions and physical sensations associated with the memory, and baseline distress ratings.

Phase 4: Desensitization

This is the core processing phase. The patient focuses briefly on the traumatic memory while simultaneously engaging in bilateral stimulation—typically guided eye movements, but sometimes alternating taps or auditory tones. Sets of bilateral stimulation last about 30 seconds. After each set, the therapist asks the patient what came up—new thoughts, images, emotions, or sensations. Processing continues until the memory no longer elicits significant distress.

Phase 5: Installation

The therapist strengthens the positive belief identified in Phase 3, linking it to the now-processed memory through additional sets of bilateral stimulation.

Phase 6: Body Scan

The patient holds the target memory and positive belief in mind while scanning their body for any residual tension or discomfort. If any is found, it is targeted with additional bilateral stimulation until it resolves.

Phase 7: Closure

Each session ends with stabilization. The therapist ensures the patient returns to a state of emotional equilibrium, using the self-regulation techniques taught in Phase 2 if needed.

Phase 8: Re-evaluation

At the start of each subsequent session, the therapist checks whether treatment gains have been maintained and whether new material has emerged that needs processing.

Why Does Bilateral Stimulation Help?

The mechanism behind EMDR is still debated, but several well-supported theories have emerged from neuroscience research.

The working memory theory, which currently has the most empirical support, proposes that the bilateral stimulation taxes the brain's working memory. Because working memory has limited capacity, holding the traumatic memory in mind while simultaneously tracking eye movements reduces the vividness and emotional intensity of the memory. Over repeated sets, the memory is reconsolidated in a less distressing form.

Research published in the Journal of Behavior Therapy and Experimental Psychiatry has shown that eye movements during recall significantly reduce both the vividness and emotionality of disturbing memories—and that this effect persists over time.

Another theory points to similarities between EMDR bilateral stimulation and the rapid eye movements that occur during REM sleep, the sleep stage most associated with memory consolidation and emotional processing. Some researchers believe EMDR may activate similar neural mechanisms to those the brain uses naturally during sleep to process and integrate emotional experiences.

What the Research Shows

The evidence base for EMDR in treating PTSD is substantial:

  • A 2020 meta-analysis published in Psychological Medicine analyzed 16 randomized controlled trials and found that EMDR produced large effect sizes in reducing PTSD symptoms, comparable to other trauma-focused therapies.
  • The VA/DoD Clinical Practice Guidelines rate EMDR as a "strongly recommended" treatment for PTSD, placing it alongside CPT and Prolonged Exposure.
  • A study of combat veterans published in the Journal of Clinical Psychology found that 77% of participants no longer met PTSD diagnostic criteria after 12 sessions of EMDR.
  • Research on single-incident trauma has shown even faster results, with some studies reporting significant improvement in as few as three to six sessions.

What EMDR Sessions Feel Like

People who have been through EMDR often describe the experience in similar ways. During processing, it is common to experience shifts in how the traumatic memory is perceived—it may feel like watching a movie rather than reliving an event. Emotions may arise and then pass relatively quickly. Some people report that connections between the traumatic memory and other life experiences become apparent during processing.

Sessions can be emotionally intense, particularly in the early stages of processing. Some people experience temporary increases in vivid dreams, emotional sensitivity, or intrusive thoughts between sessions. These are generally regarded as signs that the brain is actively processing the material, and they typically subside as treatment progresses.

Unlike some other trauma therapies, EMDR does not require detailed verbal recounting of the traumatic event. Patients need to briefly bring the memory to mind but do not have to describe it in narrative detail. This feature makes EMDR particularly appealing to people who find it difficult to talk about their trauma at length.

Who Is EMDR Appropriate For?

EMDR was originally developed for PTSD and remains most thoroughly researched for that condition. It is considered appropriate for adults with PTSD from a wide range of traumatic experiences, including combat, sexual assault, accidents, natural disasters, and childhood abuse.

It has also shown promise for related conditions including anxiety disorders, depression associated with trauma, phobias, and complicated grief. The evidence for these applications is growing but not as extensive as for PTSD.

EMDR may not be appropriate as a standalone treatment for people with active substance dependence, severe dissociative disorders, or psychotic symptoms. A thorough assessment by a trained clinician is essential before beginning treatment.

Finding an EMDR Therapist

EMDR should be administered by a licensed mental health professional who has completed an EMDRIA-approved training program. The EMDR International Association maintains a directory of certified therapists at emdria.org. When selecting a therapist, ask about their specific training, how many PTSD patients they have treated with EMDR, and their approach to the full eight-phase protocol.

EMDR Compared to Other Treatments

Head-to-head comparison studies generally find that EMDR, Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT), and Prolonged Exposure (PE) produce similar outcomes for PTSD. The choice between them often comes down to patient preference and individual circumstances.

EMDR tends to require fewer sessions than PE (which typically runs 8-15 sessions at 90 minutes each). EMDR sessions are usually 60-90 minutes. Unlike CPT, EMDR does not typically involve homework assignments between sessions, which some patients prefer. And unlike PE, EMDR does not require extended verbal retelling of the trauma narrative.

None of these therapies is universally "best." What matters most is that the treatment is evidence-based and that the patient and therapist have a good working relationship. If one approach does not produce results after an adequate trial, switching to another evidence-based treatment is a reasonable next step.

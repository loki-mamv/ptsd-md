You've heard EMDR can help with trauma. Maybe your therapist recommended it, maybe a friend swore by it, or maybe you've been reading about treatment options and this one keeps coming up. But the idea of sitting in a room while someone moves their fingers back and forth in front of your face — that sounds strange, right?

You're not wrong. EMDR looks unusual from the outside. But there's strong evidence it works, and understanding what actually happens in those first sessions can make the whole thing feel a lot less intimidating.

Here's what EMDR therapy actually looks like when you're the one in the chair.

What Is EMDR, Briefly?

EMDR stands for Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing. It was developed by psychologist Francine Shapiro in 1987 and has since become one of the most researched and recommended treatments for PTSD.

The core idea: traumatic memories get "stuck" in your brain in a way that keeps them feeling raw and present. EMDR uses bilateral stimulation — typically guided eye movements, but sometimes tapping or auditory tones — while you briefly focus on the traumatic memory. This appears to help your brain reprocess the memory so it loses its emotional charge.

A systematic narrative review of robust evidence from randomized controlled trials found that EMDR therapy improved PTSD diagnosis rates, reduced PTSD symptoms, and reduced other trauma-related symptoms including depression and anxiety (Wilson et al., 2018). The World Health Organization, the American Psychological Association, and the Department of Veterans Affairs all recommend EMDR as a frontline treatment for PTSD.

You don't need to understand the neuroscience for it to work. But knowing the basics helps demystify what's happening.

The Eight Phases of EMDR

EMDR isn't just the eye movement part. It's a structured, eight-phase protocol. Your first several sessions will likely focus on the earlier phases before any trauma processing begins.

Here's the full framework:

  1. History taking and treatment planning
  2. Preparation
  3. Assessment
  4. Desensitization
  5. Installation
  6. Body scan
  7. Closure
  8. Re-evaluation

Don't worry about memorizing these. What matters is understanding that EMDR doesn't throw you into the deep end. There's a careful setup process, and a good EMDR therapist won't rush it.

Session 1: History and Getting to Know Each Other

Your first session will feel a lot like any therapy intake. Your therapist will want to understand:

  • What brought you to therapy
  • Your trauma history (at a broad level — you won't need to go into graphic detail yet)
  • Current symptoms and how they're affecting your life
  • Your mental health history, including previous therapy and medications
  • Your support system and current life stability
  • Any history of dissociation (spacing out, feeling detached from reality, memory gaps)

That last point is important. EMDR therapists specifically screen for dissociative symptoms because the approach needs to be modified for people who dissociate significantly. This isn't a red flag — it's your therapist being thorough.

You'll also start identifying potential "target" memories — the specific traumatic experiences that are driving your current symptoms. Your therapist may ask about your earliest disturbing memory, the worst moment, and the most recent triggering event.

What it feels like: Like a thorough conversation. Some of the questions might feel uncomfortable, but you're in control of how much detail you share. A good therapist will pace this based on your comfort level.

Session 2 (Sometimes 2-3): Preparation

This is where your therapist teaches you the tools you'll need before processing begins. Think of it as building your emotional safety net.

What typically happens:

  • Explanation of EMDR: Your therapist will walk you through how it works, what to expect, and answer your questions. Good therapists welcome skepticism here.
  • Safe/calm place exercise: You'll practice visualizing a place (real or imagined) where you feel safe, calm, and at ease. This becomes an anchor you can return to if processing gets too intense.
  • Container exercise: You'll learn to mentally "contain" disturbing material that comes up — imagining putting it in a box or vault that you can set aside until your next session.
  • Bilateral stimulation practice: You'll try the eye movements (or tapping, or auditory tones) without targeting any traumatic material. This lets you get comfortable with the physical mechanics.
  • Grounding techniques: Deep breathing, body awareness, and other tools for managing distress between sessions.

Your therapist will also establish a "stop signal" — a way for you to pause or stop processing at any time. You're always in charge.

What it feels like: Like learning new skills. Some people find the calm place exercise surprisingly moving. Others feel a little silly. Both reactions are normal.

Session 3-4: Assessment and Beginning Processing

Once your therapist is confident you have adequate coping skills, you'll move into the assessment phase for your first target memory.

Choosing the target: Together, you and your therapist will identify which memory to process first. This is usually either the earliest related memory or the one causing the most distress right now. Your therapist will help you decide.

Setting up the target involves identifying:

  • The image: The most disturbing part of the memory as a mental picture
  • The negative belief: What negative thing do you believe about yourself because of this event? (Examples: "I'm not safe," "It was my fault," "I'm powerless")
  • The positive belief: What would you like to believe instead? ("I'm safe now," "I did the best I could," "I have choices")
  • Validity of Cognition (VoC): On a scale of 1-7, how true does that positive belief feel right now? (It will probably feel low — that's expected)
  • Emotions: What emotions come up when you focus on the image and the negative belief?
  • Subjective Units of Disturbance (SUD): On a scale of 0-10, how disturbing does it feel right now?
  • Body location: Where do you feel the disturbance in your body?

This part can feel intense because you're deliberately focusing on something painful. But it's brief and structured, and your therapist is watching you closely.

What Actual EMDR Processing Looks Like

Here's the part everyone wonders about.

Your therapist will ask you to hold the target memory in mind — the image, the negative belief, and the body sensation — while following a bilateral stimulus. Most commonly, this means watching your therapist's fingers move back and forth in front of your face. But many therapists also use:

  • A light bar (a moving dot of light)
  • Tapping on your hands or knees (alternating sides)
  • Headphones playing alternating tones
  • Handheld buzzers that vibrate alternately

A "set" of bilateral stimulation lasts about 20-30 seconds. After each set, your therapist will say something like, "Take a breath. What are you noticing now?" You report whatever comes up — an image, a thought, an emotion, a physical sensation. Anything.

Your therapist then says, "Go with that," and starts another set of bilateral stimulation.

This continues, with your mind often moving through the memory in unexpected ways. You might jump to a different memory, feel a surge of emotion, notice something you'd never thought about before, or gradually feel the intensity decreasing.

Important things to know:

  • You stay aware the whole time. EMDR is not hypnosis. You're conscious and in control. You can stop at any point.
  • You don't need to talk through every detail. Unlike talk therapy, EMDR doesn't require you to narrate the trauma extensively. Your brain does most of the processing internally.
  • It can get worse before it gets better — within a session. Distress often spikes during processing before it comes down. Your therapist is trained to guide you through this.
  • Sessions don't always finish in one sitting. If the processing isn't complete by the end of the session, your therapist will use the closure phase to bring you back to equilibrium before you leave.

After a Processing Session

The 24-72 hours after an EMDR processing session can be an interesting ride. It's common to experience:

  • Vivid dreams (not necessarily nightmares — sometimes just intense or unusual dreams)
  • Continued processing — new memories, thoughts, or connections surfacing
  • Feeling emotionally tired or drained
  • Feeling lighter or relieved
  • Temporary increase in emotional sensitivity

Most therapists will ask you to keep a brief log of anything that comes up between sessions. This is helpful for the re-evaluation phase at the start of your next appointment.

A meta-analysis of 26 randomized controlled trials found that EMDR produced significant improvements in PTSD symptoms, depression, anxiety, and subjective distress, with effects maintained at follow-up (Chen et al., 2014). But improvement doesn't happen overnight — and the between-session period is part of the process.

How Long Does EMDR Take?

There's no universal answer, but here are some realistic ranges:

  • Single-incident trauma (car accident, assault): 6-12 sessions total, including preparation
  • Multiple traumas or complex trauma: 12-24+ sessions, sometimes longer
  • Childhood trauma or C-PTSD: May require extensive stabilization before processing begins, with the full course lasting months to over a year

Each processing session typically lasts 60-90 minutes (longer than a standard therapy hour). Some therapists offer extended sessions specifically for EMDR processing.

Research suggests that for single-incident trauma, significant improvement can occur in as few as 3-6 processing sessions. But "quick" doesn't mean "rushed" — your therapist should always prioritize your stability over speed.

Common Concerns (and Honest Answers)

"What if I can't do the eye movements?"
No problem. Tapping, auditory tones, and handheld buzzers work too. A 2023 meta-analysis found that EMDR was effective across different forms of bilateral stimulation (Landin-Romero et al., 2023). Your therapist will find what works for you.

"Will I have to describe my trauma in detail?"
No. You need to identify the target memory and the therapist needs enough context to guide you, but you don't need to narrate the entire experience. Many people process trauma in EMDR with minimal verbal disclosure.

"What if I get overwhelmed during processing?"
This is what the preparation phase is for. You'll have a stop signal, a safe place visualization, and grounding techniques. Your therapist is also trained to notice signs of overwhelm and will adjust the pace.

"What if it doesn't work for me?"
EMDR has strong evidence behind it, but no therapy works for everyone. If EMDR isn't the right fit, other effective trauma treatments exist (CPT, Prolonged Exposure, somatic therapies). A good therapist will recognize when to pivot.

"Is it normal to feel skeptical?"
Completely. EMDR looks weird. The theory sounds almost too simple. Many people feel skeptical going in and are surprised by the results. Skepticism doesn't prevent it from working.

Finding the Right EMDR Therapist

Not all therapists who say they do EMDR have the same level of training. Here's what to look for:

  • EMDRIA certification: The EMDR International Association certifies therapists who've completed extensive training and supervised practice. This is the gold standard.
  • At minimum, Part 1 and Part 2 EMDR training from an EMDRIA-approved provider (this is the basic training requirement).
  • Experience with your type of trauma. An EMDR therapist who primarily treats car accidents may not be the best fit for complex childhood trauma, and vice versa.
  • Someone you feel comfortable with. The therapeutic relationship matters in EMDR just as much as any other therapy.

You can search the EMDRIA therapist directory or ask potential therapists about their specific EMDR training and experience.

Practical Takeaways

  • EMDR is a structured, eight-phase protocol — not just the eye movement part. Expect several sessions of preparation before processing begins.
  • Your first sessions will focus on assessment, coping skills, and building trust. No one should jump you into processing on day one.
  • You stay conscious and in control throughout the entire process. You can stop at any time.
  • You don't need to describe your trauma in detail. EMDR relies on your internal processing more than verbal narration.
  • Between-session effects are normal. Vivid dreams, new memories surfacing, and emotional ups and downs in the days after processing are part of the process.
  • Treatment length varies based on trauma complexity — from 6 sessions for single-incident trauma to months for complex trauma.
  • Look for EMDRIA-certified therapists or those with completed Part 1 and Part 2 training for the best quality care.
  • Skepticism is fine. You don't need to believe in EMDR for it to work — just be willing to try.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does EMDR therapy hurt?

EMDR isn't physically painful, but it can be emotionally intense — especially during processing sessions. You're deliberately focusing on painful memories, which can bring up strong emotions. However, the distress is temporary and typically decreases during and between sessions. Your therapist will prepare you with coping skills before processing begins.

How is EMDR different from regular talk therapy?

In traditional talk therapy, you work through trauma primarily by discussing it extensively. In EMDR, the emphasis is on internal processing guided by bilateral stimulation (eye movements, tapping, or tones). You don't need to narrate every detail of your trauma. Many people find EMDR less verbally demanding while still highly effective.

Can EMDR make PTSD worse?

When done properly by a trained therapist, EMDR is considered safe. Some people experience temporary increases in distress during or between processing sessions — this is a normal part of the process, not a worsening of PTSD. However, EMDR that's poorly administered or rushed without adequate preparation can be destabilizing, which is why therapist qualifications matter.

Is EMDR only for PTSD?

EMDR was originally developed for PTSD and has the strongest evidence base for trauma. However, it's also being used for anxiety, depression, phobias, grief, chronic pain, and other conditions. The evidence for non-PTSD uses is growing but less established than for trauma.

Can I do EMDR through telehealth?

Yes. Many therapists adapted EMDR for virtual sessions during and after the pandemic. Instead of following the therapist's hand, you might watch a moving dot on screen or use self-tapping (butterfly hug). Research on virtual EMDR is still emerging, but early results suggest it can be effective. Ask your therapist about their telehealth EMDR protocol.

PTSD Treatment Options

Updated Feb 2026 · 15 min read

PTSD is treatable. Research shows that most people who complete evidence-based treatment see significant improvement. Here's what works and what to expect.

Key Takeaways

  • Trauma-focused psychotherapy is the most effective treatment
  • 53-80% of people no longer meet PTSD criteria after completing treatment
  • Medications can help but work best combined with therapy
  • Treatment involves processing trauma, not just managing symptoms

Trauma-Focused Psychotherapies

These are the most effective treatments. They involve directly addressing the trauma rather than just managing symptoms.

Prolonged Exposure (PE)

Strongly Recommended

PE helps you gradually approach trauma-related memories, feelings, and situations you've been avoiding. Through repeated, controlled exposure, the memories lose their power to cause distress.

What happens:

  • Psychoeducation about PTSD and treatment
  • Breathing retraining for relaxation
  • In vivo exposure: Gradually facing avoided situations in real life
  • Imaginal exposure: Revisiting the trauma memory in session (recorded for homework)

Duration: Typically 8-15 sessions of 90 minutes

Evidence: Decades of research. Effective for combat, sexual assault, accidents, and other trauma types.

Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT)

Strongly Recommended

CPT helps identify and challenge unhelpful thoughts and beliefs that developed after trauma ("stuck points"). These might include beliefs like "It was my fault," "I can never be safe," or "No one can be trusted."

What happens:

  • Writing an impact statement about what the trauma means to you
  • Learning to identify stuck points
  • Using worksheets to challenge and modify unhelpful beliefs
  • Addressing themes: safety, trust, power/control, esteem, intimacy

Duration: Usually 12 sessions of 60 minutes

Variations: Can be done with or without a written trauma account. Group format also effective.

EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing)

Recommended

EMDR uses bilateral stimulation (usually eye movements, but can be taps or sounds) while processing traumatic memories. The exact mechanism isn't fully understood, but it appears to help the brain reprocess stuck memories.

What happens:

  • History-taking and treatment planning
  • Learning coping skills for distress
  • Identifying target memories and associated negative beliefs
  • Desensitization: Processing memories while following eye movements
  • Installing positive beliefs to replace negative ones

Duration: Typically 6-12 sessions

Note: May require less direct talking about trauma details than PE, which some find appealing.

Medications

Medications don't cure PTSD but can reduce symptoms, making therapy more tolerable and effective.

FDA-Approved for PTSD

  • Sertraline (Zoloft): SSRI antidepressant. Helps with all symptom clusters.
  • Paroxetine (Paxil): SSRI antidepressant. Similar effectiveness to sertraline.

Other Commonly Used Medications

  • Prazosin: Blood pressure medication that reduces nightmares. Often very effective for sleep.
  • Venlafaxine (Effexor): SNRI antidepressant with good evidence for PTSD.
  • Other SSRIs/SNRIs: Fluoxetine, citalopram, and others are sometimes used.

⚠️ Medications to Approach Cautiously

Benzodiazepines (Xanax, Klonopin, Ativan) may provide short-term relief but can worsen PTSD outcomes long-term and carry addiction risk. They're generally not recommended for PTSD.

What About Newer Treatments?

MDMA-Assisted Therapy

Currently in Phase 3 trials and showing promising results. Uses MDMA in controlled therapeutic sessions to facilitate trauma processing. Not yet FDA-approved but may become available in coming years.

Stellate Ganglion Block (SGB)

An injection that blocks certain nerve signals. Some evidence for rapid symptom reduction, but more research needed. Not yet standard treatment.

Ketamine

Being studied for PTSD, particularly treatment-resistant cases. Some clinics offer it, but evidence is still emerging.

How to Choose a Treatment

The good news: PE, CPT, and EMDR have similar effectiveness. Consider:

  • Therapist availability: What trained therapists are near you or available online?
  • Your preferences: Do you want to talk through the trauma in detail (PE) or focus more on beliefs (CPT)?
  • Practical factors: Session length (PE is 90 min, CPT is 60 min) and homework requirements

What to Expect

Treatment typically involves:

  1. Assessment: Understanding your symptoms and trauma history
  2. Preparation: Learning about PTSD and building coping skills
  3. Processing: Working through trauma memories and beliefs
  4. Integration: Applying insights to daily life

It may get harder before it gets easier. Processing trauma can temporarily increase distress. This is normal and expected. A good therapist will pace treatment appropriately and help you manage symptoms.

Homework matters. All evidence-based treatments involve practice between sessions. People who do the homework consistently have better outcomes.

Finding a Trauma Therapist

  • Ask about training: Have they been trained in PE, CPT, or EMDR specifically?
  • Veterans: VA offers free PTSD treatment. Community Vet Centers also available.
  • Directories: Psychology Today, EMDR International Association, and PTSD Foundation have therapist finders
  • Telehealth: Many trauma therapies are effective via video—expands your options

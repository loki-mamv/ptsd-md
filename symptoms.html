PTSD Symptoms

PTSD symptoms fall into four clusters. For a diagnosis, symptoms must persist for more than a month and significantly impact daily life.

Diagnostic Criteria Overview

To be diagnosed with PTSD, you must have experienced or witnessed a traumatic event AND have symptoms from each of the four clusters below for more than one month, causing significant distress or impairment.

ðŸ”„ Cluster B: Intrusion / Re-experiencing

The trauma keeps coming back uninvited. Need at least one of:

  • Intrusive memories: Unwanted, distressing memories of the trauma that pop up spontaneously
  • Nightmares: Distressing dreams related to the traumatic event
  • Flashbacks: Feeling or acting as if the trauma is happening again. The most severe formâ€”you may lose awareness of your current surroundings
  • Psychological distress: Intense emotional reactions when reminded of the trauma
  • Physical reactions: Racing heart, sweating, nausea when reminded

ðŸš« Cluster C: Avoidance

Actively avoiding anything related to the trauma. Need at least one of:

  • Internal avoidance: Efforts to avoid distressing memories, thoughts, or feelings about the trauma
  • External avoidance: Efforts to avoid people, places, activities, objects, or situations that trigger memories

Avoidance feels protective but maintains PTSD by preventing the brain from processing the memory normally.

ðŸ’­ Cluster D: Negative Cognitions & Mood

Changes in thoughts and feelings after the trauma. Need at least two of:

  • Memory gaps: Inability to remember important aspects of the trauma (not due to head injury or substances)
  • Negative beliefs: Persistent, distorted beliefs about yourself, others, or the world ("I'm broken," "No one can be trusted," "The world is completely dangerous")
  • Distorted blame: Persistent self-blame or blame of others for causing the trauma or its consequences
  • Persistent negative emotions: Ongoing fear, horror, anger, guilt, or shame
  • Diminished interest: Markedly decreased interest in activities that used to matter
  • Detachment: Feeling distant or cut off from others
  • Inability to feel positive: Persistent inability to experience happiness, satisfaction, or loving feelings

âš¡ Cluster E: Arousal & Reactivity

Changes in how alert and reactive you are. Need at least two of:

  • Irritability: Irritable behavior and angry outbursts with little provocation
  • Reckless behavior: Self-destructive or reckless actions
  • Hypervigilance: Being on constant alert for danger
  • Exaggerated startle: Jumping at sounds or movements others ignore
  • Concentration problems: Difficulty focusing or paying attention
  • Sleep disturbance: Difficulty falling or staying asleep

Related Conditions

Complex PTSD (C-PTSD)

Not yet an official diagnosis in the US (DSM-5) but recognized by the WHO (ICD-11). Develops from prolonged, repeated traumaâ€”often during childhood. In addition to PTSD symptoms, includes:

  • Difficulty regulating emotions
  • Negative self-concept and chronic shame
  • Difficulty in relationships and trust

Acute Stress Disorder

Similar symptoms to PTSD but lasting less than one month after trauma. May or may not develop into PTSD. Treatment can prevent progression.

What Symptoms Are NOT

PTSD symptoms are not:

  • Weakness or character flaws. PTSD is a neurobiological response to overwhelming stress.
  • Permanent. With treatment, symptoms significantly improve or resolve for most people.
  • "Going crazy." These are normal reactions to abnormal events.
  • Your fault. You didn't choose to develop PTSD.

ðŸš¨ When to Seek Help Immediately

Get help right away if you're having thoughts of suicide or self-harm, experiencing severe flashbacks or dissociation, or using substances to cope. Call 988 (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline) or go to your nearest emergency room.

Do I Have PTSD?

Only a qualified mental health professional can diagnose PTSD. If you recognize several of these symptoms in yourself after a traumatic experience, it's worth seeking an evaluation. Early treatment leads to better outcomes.

Many people experience some trauma symptoms in the weeks after a traumatic event. This is normal. The concern is when symptoms persist beyond a month and interfere with your life.
